ABOUT LANGKAWI

Langkawi is one of Malaysia's top vacation spots. Langkawi has beautiful beaches, sky-blue oceans, and lots of nature for tourists to enjoy. It has 99 islands, but Langkawi is the biggest one. It is in the state of Kedah, 50 km west of Peninsular Malaysia's northwest coast. Langkawi is home to a vast number of different animals and plants. It was the first park in Southeast Asia to be named a UNESCO World Geopark in 2007. The oldest mountain range in Malaysia is Langkawi, where the first rock formations were found to be 550 million years old.

PLACES TO GO

Langkawi Geopark

There are over 90 Langkawi geoparks spread across the island of Langkawi's 99 islands, but three stand out in particular:


Kilim Karst Geoforest Park

It was the result of erosion over millions of years, which formed mangrove forests, cliffs, caves, and other karstic formations. It's in the northeast of Langkawi. The yellow sea hibiscus, the limestone cycad that grows on the cliff faces, and the island's sea eagle are all rare plants and animals found at the park. You can only get there by boat. The park was named after the river, Kilim River.


Machinchang Cambrian Geoforest Park

The Machinchang mountain range is the oldest in all of Malaysia. The rocks were mostly made of sandstone or granite, dated earliest from the Cambrian period. This part of Langkawi is in the northwest. From the Langkawi SkyBridge, you can able to see it. This place is what remains of the pre-historic rainforest and is evidence of erosion and weathering.


Dayang Bunting Marble Geoforest Park

It is located on Langkawi's second-largest island. From Langkawi's main island, you can get there by boat. The name "Daynang Bunting" comes from the shape of the island's hills, which resembled a "pregnant maiden." A lake was made when a giant limestone cave collapsed because of erosion.
